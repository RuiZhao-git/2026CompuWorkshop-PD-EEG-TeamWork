# Cortical EEG slowing in resting-state Parkinson's disease

# Hypothesis: cortical EEG slowing in resting-state PD

## How we narrowed down

1. **Initial hypothesis**: increased cortical beta activity in PD, based on prior literature describing elevated beta oscillations in PD.
2. **Further reading clarified** that this finding is reported primarily in intracranial / subthalamic nucleus recordings and reflects basal ganglia motor circuit pathology. Scalp EEG measures cortical activity and represents a separate signal.
3. **In cortical resting-state EEG**, PD has been associated with a shift of spectral power toward lower frequencies, referred to as "EEG slowing".

## Final hypothesis

> Compared to age-matched healthy controls, Parkinson's disease patients at rest show:
> - decreased alpha (8-13 Hz) power
> - increased theta (4-8 Hz) power
> - decreased cortical beta (13-30 Hz) power

<!-- _footer: Consistent with the spectral findings reported in Anjum et al., 2024, npj Parkinson's Disease. -->

# Proposed model fitting approach

## Model

A minimal cortical oscillator with two coupled neural populations, excitatory (E) and inhibitory (I). Their interaction produces a resting alpha rhythm. We use the Wilson-Cowan model as a representative example.

![h:200](figures/05_slides/ei_schematic.svg)

## Free parameter and fitting

- **Free parameter**: the inhibitory time constant τᵢ controls oscillation speed and connects directly to the hypothesised alpha slowing.
- **Healthy and PD models**: parameters chosen so simulated alpha peaks match the Control and PD group medians; the Δτᵢ corresponds to the "+x" in the assignment diagram.
- **Per-subject fitting**: extract each participant's alpha peak from their resting EEG, then find the τᵢ that reproduces it in the model.
- **Group comparison**: compare τᵢ distributions between PD and Control groups.

## Note on cortical beta

This minimal model directly captures alpha and theta. Cortical beta is consistent with the general slowing pattern but would require a second oscillator to model explicitly.
